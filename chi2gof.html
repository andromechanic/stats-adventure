01

The Big Idea

Statistical Reality Check

The Chi-Square Goodness of Fit test is a statistical powerhouse used to determine if your observed data matches what you "expected" to see.

You have a theory about how the data should be spread out (the Expected distribution), and you compare it to the data you actually collected (the Observed distribution).

Core Formula

$$ \chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i} $$

$O_i$: Observed $E_i$: Expected $\sum$: Summation
02

Golden Rules of Fit

Categorical Data

The data must be counts or frequencies (not percentages, means, or ratios).

Independence

Each observation must be independent (one person's choice doesn't affect another's).

Sample Size Rule

Each Expected frequency ($E_i$) should be at least 5 to avoid a "jittery" test.

Degrees of Freedom

Calculated as $df = k - 1$, where $k$ is the number of categories.

Three Flavors of Goodness

Case Study 01

Uniform: The "Fairness" Test

Assumption:
Every outcome equal

Scenario: You suspect a six-sided die is "loaded" (unfair). You roll it 60 times.

Expected ($E$): 10 per side (60 ÷ 6)

Observed ($O$): 20 rolls of '6', 2 rolls of '1'

The Result

A high $\chi^2$ value would lead you to reject the "fair die" hypothesis.

Case Study 02

Proportions: The "M&M" Test

Assumption:
Preset Percentages
Color Red (20%) Blue (20%) Yellow (60%)
Expected 20 20 60
Observed 35 15 50
Goal: Check if the variation in your 100-candy bag is "random luck" or if the company is lying about their color ratios.
Case Study 03

Normal: The "Bell Curve" Test

Assumption:
Normal Distribution

Scenario: Exam Scores

  1. Divide scores into "bins" (e.g., 50-60, 60-70).
  2. Use the Normal formula to see how many should fall into each bin.
  3. Compare expectations to the actual student counts.

Advanced Field Reports

01. Genetics

Mendelian Inheritance

Theory: 3:1 phenotypic ratio (75% Round, 25% Wrinkled).

Reality: scientist grows 400 plants. Sees 280 Round instead of 300.

Goal: Determine if a biological factor (like a lethal gene) is interfering with random chance.

02. Business

Customer Arrivals

Theory: Poisson Distribution (constant average rate).

Observation: Track bank entries in 5-min windows for a month.

Goal: If data doesn't fit, customers might arrive in "bursts" (e.g., buses), requiring new staffing.

03. Psychology

Preference Testing

Theory: 50/50 Binomial Distribution (no preference).

Test: 100 people choose between Sodas X and Y.

Goal: Is 62/100 different enough to claim a winner, or just a fluke?

Selection Guide: Which Distribution?

Scenario Expected Use Case
Fairness/Equality Uniform Checking dice or accidents per weekday.
Industry Standards Proportional Comparing bag contents to advertised ratios.
Natural Phenomena Normal Checking if heights or scores fit a bell curve.
Rare Events Poisson Checking randomness of typos or soccer goals.

A Quick Note on $df$

$df = k - 1$

Sodas (2)

$df = 1$

Plants (2)

$df = 1$

Die Rolls (6)

$df = 5$

The more categories you have, the higher the $\chi^2$ value needs to be before you can confidently say the "fit" is bad.
