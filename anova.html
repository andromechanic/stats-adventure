Multi-Group Comparison

ANOVA
(Analysis of Variance)

Measuring "Between" vs. "Within" to determine if three or more independent groups have truly different means.

01

The Core Logic

While a t-test compares the means of exactly two groups, ANOVA allows you to test multiple groups simultaneously without increasing the risk of a "Type I error" (False Positive). Crucially, ANOVA doesn't compare means directly—it compares variances.

Between-Group

The Signal

How much do the means of the different groups differ from each other?

Within-Group

The Noise

How much do individual scores inside each group differ from that group's mean?

The F-Ratio Engine

\[ F = \frac{\text{Variance Between Groups}}{\text{Variance Within Groups}} \]

High F = Differences are likely REAL. Low F = Differences are just NOISE.

Variations of ANOVA

  • 1.

    One-Way: One independent variable. (e.g. Fertilizer Brand vs. Height)

  • 2.

    Two-Way: Two independent variables. (e.g. Fertilizer Brand AND Sunlight vs. Height)

  • 3.

    MANOVA: Multiple dependent variables at once.

The Assumptions

  • ✓

    Normality: Each group data must follow a bell curve.

  • ✓

    Homogeneity: Variance (spread) should be roughly equal across groups.

  • ✓

    Independence: One plant's growth doesn't affect another's.

04

The Gardener's Example

A gardener measures 15 tomato plants across 3 Fertilizer Brands (A, B, C). ANOVA calculates that Brand C is significantly taller than A and B.

Calculated P-Value

0.01

Conclusion: Since \(0.01 < 0.05\), we Reject the Null (\(H_0\)). The fertilizers are NOT all the same.

The "Omnibus" Test Warning

ANOVA tells you that there is a difference, but it doesn't tell you where it is. Our test proves Brands are different overall, but doesn't explicitly prove Brand C is the best.
