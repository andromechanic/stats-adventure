Quantifying the "Average" or "Middle" of a Dataset. Once you have a center, you can measure the Spread.

01

The Arithmetic Mean (\(\bar{x}\))

The sum of all values divided by the total number of items.

\( \bar{x} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n} \)

The Balance Point of the Data

Key Characteristic

Highly sensitive to Outliers. A single extremely high value can pull the mean away from the "typical" center.

02

The Median (\(M\))

The exact "Middle" value when data is sorted in order.

Finding the Middle

  • 1. Sort the dataset in ascending order.
  • 2. If n is odd: The middle number.
  • 3. If n is even: The average of the two middle numbers.
12 15 18 21 45

Resistance to Outliers

"The Median is robust—it doesn't care if that 45 becomes 4,500."

03

The Mode

The value that appears most frequently in a dataset. Unlike Mean/Median, it can be used for Categorical data.

Unimodal

One single peak (most common).

Bimodal

Two distinct frequencies are tied for highest.

Multimodal

Many frequent peaks or no unique mode.

04

Visualizing Skewness

Symmetrical

Mean = Median = Mode

Right Skewed (+)

Mode < Median < Mean

In right-skewed data (like income), the high values pull the Mean furthest to the right.

The Statistical Verdict

When to Use?

M

Use Mean for normal data without severe outliers (e.g. Test scores).

M

Use Median for skewed data (e.g. Housing prices, Income).
