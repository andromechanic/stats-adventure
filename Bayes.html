The Geometry of Changing Beliefs based on Conditional Probability.

01

Core Concept

New evidence should not determine your beliefs; it should update your prior beliefs.

02

The "Steve" Example

The Setup

"Steve is shy and withdrawn, invariably helpful but with very little interest in people... a meek and tidy soul, he has a need for order and structure."

Question: Is Steve a Librarian or a Farmer?

The Common Error

Most people choose Librarian because the description fits a stereotype. This is irrational because it ignores the Base Rate.

Rational Thinking with the Missing Factor:

  • The Base Rate: There are roughly 20 farmers for every 1 librarian.
  • The Logic: Even if a librarian is 4x more likely to be "tidy," the sheer volume of farmers means there are still more "tidy farmers" in total.
Representative Sample
200 Farmers : 10 Librarians
03

Geometry of Probabilities

Instead of memorizing numbers, imagine a 1x1 Square representing all possibilities.

H (True)
Not H
1

Prior \( P(H) \): Split the square vertically based on your initial belief (e.g., Librarian vs Farmer ratio).

2

Likelihood \( P(E|H) \): Shade a horizontal portion of the "True" side for those who fit the evidence.

3

The Update: Once evidence \( E \) is seen, the unshaded space "disappears." Your new probability is the proportion of the shaded Librarian area over the total shaded area.

04

The Formula Breakdown

\[ \color{#9333ea}{P(H|E)} = \frac{\color{#2563eb}{P(H)} \cdot \color{#16a34a}{P(E|H)}}{\color{#ca8a04}{P(E)}} \]
\( P(H|E) \) Posterior:

The probability the hypothesis is true given the evidence.

\( P(H) \) Prior:

Your initial belief before seeing any evidence.

\( P(E|H) \) Likelihood:

Probability of seeing this evidence if the hypothesis were true.

\( P(E) \) Total Evidence:

Total probability of seeing evidence across all cases (both \( H \) and \( \neg H \)).

05

Takeaways for Intuition

Think in Counts, Not Percentages

Humans estimate better with "4 out of 100" than "4%." Phrasing problems as representative samples (e.g., 210 people) eliminates most logical fallacies.

The "Linda" Problem (Conjunction Fallacy)

85% of people incorrectly say it's more likely Linda is a "feminist bank teller" than just a "bank teller."

Error rate drops from 85% to 0% when you ask for counts (e.g., "How many out of 100?").

Irrelevant Evidence

If the likelihood \( P(E|H) \) is the same as \( P(E|\neg H) \), the evidence is useless and your belief shouldn't change at all.
